Your Legal Rights — United States
Plain-language guide for survivors of domestic violence and human trafficking in the U.S.
Module 04 — Legal
Template: BNB-T04c v1.0 · 2026
This is general legal information, not legal advice. Laws vary by state. Speak with a qualified attorney or your state's legal aid office. Contact National DV Hotline: 1-800-799-7233 (TTY: 1-800-787-3224) · thehotline.org. National Human Trafficking Hotline: 1-888-373-7888.
1. Protective / Restraining Orders
Emergency Protective Order (EPO) — Police can issue an EPO on the spot. It typically lasts 3–7 days and gives you immediate protection.
Temporary Restraining Order (TRO) — You can get a TRO from a courthouse without the abuser present. It usually lasts 20–25 days until a full hearing.
Permanent Restraining Order — After a court hearing (where you can bring evidence and witnesses), a judge can issue a long-term order (1–5 years, sometimes longer).
VINE (Victim Information & Notification Everyday) — Register at vinelink.com to be notified if the abuser is released from custody. It is free.
Violation of an order — If the abuser violates the order, call 911 immediately. Violation is a criminal offense.
2. VAWA — Violence Against Women Act
VAWA Self-Petition (Immigration) — If you are married to a U.S. citizen or permanent resident who has abused you, you may be able to apply for a green card independently ("self-petition"), without the abuser's knowledge or cooperation.
Confidentiality — USCIS is required by law to keep your VAWA application strictly confidential. The abuser cannot find out.
Who is eligible — Spouses, children, and parents of abusive U.S. citizens or permanent residents may be eligible. Contact a VAWA-certified legal advocate for assessment.
T and U Visas — If you were trafficked (T visa) or are a victim of certain crimes and have been helpful to law enforcement (U visa), you may qualify for legal status and work authorization.
3. Criminal Process
You do not have to press charges alone — In many states, the state (prosecutor) can charge the abuser even if you choose not to cooperate. You will have a Victim Advocate to support you.
Your Victim Rights — Under the Crime Victims' Rights Act (federal) and most state laws, you have the right to be notified of proceedings, to be heard at sentencing, to be protected from the offender, and to receive restitution.
Victim Advocate — The DA's office or a DV organization can provide a free victim advocate to accompany you to court, explain the process, and help you understand your rights.
4. Housing Rights
Early lease termination — Most states allow DV survivors to break a lease early without penalty, with documentation (police report, protective order, or a statement from an advocate).
Public housing protections — The federal Violence Against Women Act prohibits public housing authorities from evicting you because of domestic violence, dating violence, sexual assault, or stalking.
Emergency shelter vouchers — Ask Beacon staff about emergency vouchers available in your area through HUD or local programs.
5. Benefits & Financial Support
TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families) — Cash assistance for families with children. DV survivors may qualify for a waiver of work requirements if participation in the work program would put you at risk.
SNAP (Food Stamps) — You may qualify for food assistance. Apply online at your state's benefits portal or at your local DHS office.
Medicaid — Free or low-cost health insurance if you meet income requirements. Emergency Medicaid is available for undocumented individuals for emergency care only.
Crime Victim Compensation — Most states have a fund to reimburse crime victims for out-of-pocket expenses (medical, counseling, lost wages). Ask Beacon staff or a victim advocate about applying.
Child Support Enforcement — The state can help you collect child support from a non-paying parent. Contact your local child support agency.
