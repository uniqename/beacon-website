Your Legal Rights — Ghana
Plain-language guide for survivors of domestic violence and human trafficking in Ghana
Module 04 — Legal
Template: BNB-T04b v1.0 · 2026
This is general legal information, not legal advice. Every case is different. Speak with a qualified lawyer or contact our legal partner for advice on your specific situation. This guide was accurate as of 2026 — laws can change.
1. The Domestic Violence Act (Act 732, 2007)

Ghana's Domestic Violence Act makes it illegal for anyone — including a spouse, partner, or family member — to physically, emotionally, sexually, or economically abuse you.

What counts as domestic violence — physical harm, sexual violence (including by a spouse), emotional / psychological abuse, economic abuse (controlling your money or property), and harmful traditional practices.
You have the right to report — Go to any DOVVSU (Domestic Violence and Victim Support Unit) office to make a report. You do not need a lawyer to report.
Protection orders — A court can issue a Protection Order telling the abuser to stay away from you and your children. You or a social worker can apply on your behalf. Contact Beacon's legal partner for help.
The abuser can be arrested — Police can arrest the perpetrator without a warrant if there is danger to you or your children.
Criminal prosecution — The state can prosecute the abuser. You may be asked to give evidence, but you will be supported through this process.
2. Human Trafficking (Anti-Human Trafficking Act, Act 694, 2005)

It is illegal in Ghana to recruit, transport, transfer, harbour, or receive a person for the purposes of exploitation.

You are not a criminal — If you were trafficked, you are a victim, not a criminal. You have the right to protection and support.
Report to the police or Human Trafficking Unit — Call the Human Trafficking Hotline: 0800-111-222 (update this number for your region).
Repatriation support — If you were trafficked from another region or country, you have the right to be safely returned home with support.
Compensation — Courts can order traffickers to pay compensation to victims.
3. Marriage & Divorce Rights
Types of marriage in Ghana — Ordinance (court/church), customary, and Islamic marriages are all legally recognised. Your rights differ slightly depending on which type you have.
Divorce — You have the right to divorce. In an ordinance marriage, you apply to the court. In a customary marriage, the process is through the family and/or traditional authorities, but a court can also be used.
Marital property — Under Ghana's Intestate Succession Law and case law, a spouse (including women) has rights to jointly acquired property. Speak to a lawyer about your specific situation.
Forced marriage — Forcing someone to marry is illegal. If you were forced into marriage or are being forced to stay in one, report it to DOVVSU or the police.
4. Child Custody & Maintenance
Your children's best interest — Courts in Ghana make custody decisions based on the best interest of the child. Abuse of a parent is a factor courts consider.
Child maintenance — A father (or parent) is legally required to provide financial support for their children. The court can make a maintenance order.
Custody orders — If you are worried the abuser may take your children, apply for a custody order immediately. Beacon's legal partner can assist.
5. Identity Documents
Ghana Card (National ID) — You have the right to apply for a Ghana Card at any NIA office. It is free. Ask Beacon staff if you need help with this process.
Birth certificate for your children — You can register your child's birth at any Births and Deaths Registry. This is free and does not require the father's signature if he is absent or abusive.
Lost or withheld documents — If the abuser has taken or is withholding your documents, speak to Beacon staff. We can help you apply for replacements.
