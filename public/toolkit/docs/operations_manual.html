This is a living manual. Adapt all sections to your local context, laws, and building. Review annually or after any critical incident. All staff must read and sign that they have understood this manual before their first day.
Part 1 — Shelter Setup & Physical Safety

1.1 Location Security

The shelter's physical address must be kept confidential. It should not appear on letterheads, social media, or any public-facing materials. Staff must not share the address with anyone who is not an authorised Beacon staff member, partner, or resident.

Entry protocols — All entrances must be controlled. The main door should remain locked at all times and only opened after visual confirmation of identity (intercom or camera). A buzzer or bell system is required.
Security systems — CCTV at entry points (not in private areas), motion-activated outdoor lighting, and at minimum one working landline or emergency device that functions without internet.
Safe room — Identify one room in the shelter that can be locked from the inside and has a phone. Brief residents and staff on its location on Day 1.
Fire safety — Functional smoke detectors in all rooms, fire extinguisher on each floor, clearly posted evacuation routes. Monthly fire drill (date it in the log).

Part 4 — Intake Process
Step 1 — First contact — Referral received (by phone, hotline, partner, self-referral). Collect basic information: safety status, immediate needs, number of children. Do NOT ask about the abuse history at this stage.
Step 2 — Safety check before arrival — Complete phone-based digital safety check (BNB-07a). Ensure the person is not traveling directly from the abuser. Coordinate safe transport if needed.
Step 3 — Arrival and welcome — Greet the person warmly. Show them their room, bathroom, and common areas. Give them a welcome pack (toiletries, information sheet, emergency numbers, house rules). Do not immediately start paperwork.
Step 4 — Intake paperwork (within 24 hours) — Complete the Resident Intake Form (BNB-02b) at a calm moment, not immediately upon arrival. Explain the consent section fully. Give them a copy.
Step 5 — Needs assessment (within 48 hours) — Conduct the Survivor Assessment (BNB-03a) and Risk/Safety Assessment (BNB-03b). Begin identifying immediate service needs.
Step 6 — Shelter checklist (staff only) — Complete the Shelter Safety Checklist (BNB-02a) for all rooms and facilities. Ensure the space is safe before the person arrives.
Part 5 — Crisis Response & De-escalation
Stay calm — Speak slowly and quietly. Lower your own body language. Sit rather than stand. Do not block exits.
Validate the emotion — "I can see you're really upset. That makes complete sense. I want to help."
Give space — If the person needs space, offer to step back. Stay within visual range but give physical distance.
Involve the resident in decisions — "What would help right now?" Never impose a solution. Survivor agency is paramount even in a crisis.
When to call emergency services — Call immediately if there is a risk of serious physical harm to the resident, another resident, a child, or a staff member. Document in the incident report (BNB-02c) within 2 hours.
After a crisis — Debrief with staff. Check in with any other residents who witnessed it. Offer the survivor a follow-up conversation with their Case Manager within 24 hours. Submit incident report.
Part 6 — Discharge & Transitions
Planned discharge — Begin discharge planning from Week 4. Use BNB-02d (Discharge Plan). Ideal discharge = resident has housing, income or income plan, safety plan, and active referrals in place.
Maximum stay — Standard maximum stay is . Extensions require Program Coordinator approval and documented justification.
Emergency discharge — If a resident must leave due to a serious safety or conduct breach, follow the emergency discharge protocol. Give written notice. Connect to another shelter if possible. Never discharge into homelessness without exhausting all alternatives.
Follow-up — Contact all residents at 1 month and 3 months post-discharge. Record in BNB-03e (Case Closure). Track housing, safety, and employment status.
Part 7 — Complaints Procedure

Every resident has the right to complain about any aspect of the service without fear of retaliation. This right must be explained clearly at intake.

Informal complaint — Speak to any staff member. They will try to resolve it immediately or refer to the Case Manager.
Formal written complaint — Written complaints must be acknowledged within 48 hours and fully responded to within 10 working days. The Program Coordinator investigates all formal complaints.
Complaints about the Program Coordinator — Escalated directly to the Executive Director / Board.
Safeguarding complaints — Any complaint involving harm to a resident, child, or vulnerable person is referred immediately to the Safeguarding Lead and statutory authorities as required by law.
