Module 04 — Guide for case managers on legal processes, evidence, and referrals
Module 04 — Legal
BNB-DOC-04 Site: v1.0 · 2026
For case manager use — not for distribution to survivors without review. This pack guides staff on how to support survivors through legal processes. It does not constitute legal advice. Always connect survivors to the site's qualified legal partner for case-specific advice.
Part 1 — Case Manager's Role in Legal Advocacy

As a Case Manager, your legal advocacy role is to support — not lead — the survivor's legal journey. You are not a lawyer. Your role is to:

Inform — Explain the legal options available in plain language using the survivor's legal rights guide (BNB-T04b / BNB-T04c). Never pressure a survivor toward any particular legal action.
Document — Help the survivor record evidence of abuse using the Evidence Log (BNB-04a). Explain how to preserve and store evidence safely (see Part 3 below).
Refer — Complete the Legal Referral Form (BNB-04b) and connect the survivor to the site's legal partner when the survivor consents. Follow up.
Accompany — With the survivor's consent, attend court dates, police stations, or legal appointments as a support person. You are there for emotional support, not to speak on their behalf.
Coordinate — Liaise between the legal partner, the survivor, and other service providers (housing, childcare) to ensure the legal process does not create safety risks.
Part 2 — Key Legal Pathways

2.1 Protection / Restraining Orders

This is often the first legal step and the most immediately protective. Work with the legal partner to assist the survivor in applying. Key principles:

Application can usually be made without the abuser being present (ex parte application).
A temporary order is typically issued quickly; a permanent order follows a hearing where both parties can attend.
If the order is violated, instruct the survivor to call police immediately and provide a copy of the order to police.
Help the survivor store copies of the order in multiple safe locations (with their case manager, in the safe, with a trusted person outside the shelter).

2.2 Police Reports

Survivors have the right to report at any time. The case manager should not pressure them to report; support whatever decision they make.
Accompany the survivor to the police station if requested. Bring documentation (incident records, photos, medical records).
Get a case number and copy of the report before leaving. Store in the case file (locked, confidential).

2.3 Criminal Prosecution

In many jurisdictions, once a report is made, the state/prosecution decides whether to charge — the survivor cannot "drop charges" in the traditional sense.
Prosecutors may ask the survivor to give evidence. Support them through this process with a victim advocate.
The survivor can request protection measures during the trial (e.g., video link testimony, screens in court). Help them ask for this through the legal partner.

2.4 Divorce and Family Law

Connect the survivor to the legal partner for divorce proceedings — do not attempt to advise on this yourself.
Custody applications can be made at the same time. Note: the existence of a protection order against a parent is significant in custody proceedings.
For issues of child maintenance, the family court or maintenance court can enforce payment.
Part 3 — Evidence Documentation Guide
All evidence collection must be done with the survivor's informed consent. Never collect evidence on their behalf without their knowledge.
Photos of injuries — Take clear photos with good lighting. Include a ruler for scale where possible. Take full-body photos plus close-ups. Capture injuries on all visible skin areas. Document date, time, and location in the Evidence Log (BNB-04a). Store encrypted or printed and locked.
Screenshots and digital evidence — Help the survivor screenshot threatening messages, emails, or social media posts. Include metadata (date/time visible). Back up to a secure, non-shared location. Make printed copies as backup.
Medical records — Encourage the survivor to request copies of medical treatment for injuries. These are their records; they have the right to them. Store with the case file.
Witness information — If there are witnesses (neighbours, school staff, family members), record their contact information in the Evidence Log. The legal partner will decide whether to interview them formally.
The Beacon app Evidence Tool — The Beacon app has a secure, encrypted evidence feature that time-stamps photos and recordings. This is designed to be court-admissible. Walk the survivor through using it if it is safe for them to do so (check digital safety assessment first).
Chain of custody — Once evidence is collected, record who has it, where it is stored, and who has access. Do not share with anyone outside the legal team without the survivor's consent.
Part 4 — Confidentiality & Data Protection
No information without consent — Never share a survivor's information, location, case details, or legal status with anyone — including family members, police, or other agencies — without their explicit written consent. The only exception is mandatory reporting requirements under local law (safeguarding of children, serious risk to life).
Mandatory reporting — If you believe a child is in immediate danger, you are required by law to report to child protection authorities. Explain this to the survivor as part of intake so there are no surprises. Fill in the mandatory reporting section of BNB-02b at intake. If this situation arises, consult the Program Coordinator immediately.
Perpetrator inquiries — If a perpetrator contacts the shelter directly, do not confirm or deny whether the survivor is there. Do not engage. End the call. Report to the Program Coordinator and record in the incident log.
Subpoenas and court orders — If the organisation receives a subpoena for case records, immediately contact your legal partner before releasing anything. Case notes enjoy a degree of privilege in most jurisdictions, but this varies.
