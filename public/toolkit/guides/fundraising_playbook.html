Part 1 — Building Your Funding Strategy

1.1 The Diversification Rule

Never rely on a single funder for more than 30% of your annual budget. If that funder stops or reduces their grant, you lose a third of your program. Healthy diversification looks like:

Part 2 — Grant Writing Guide

2.1 Identifying Funders

Local and national foundations — Search for foundations in your country that fund DV, women's rights, homelessness, or social services. Use foundation websites, network with other NGOs, and check government grant databases.
International funders — Key international funders active in DV/GBV work: USAID, UN Women, Comic Relief, Oak Foundation, Wellspring Philanthropic Fund, NoVo Foundation. Always check their current geographic and thematic priorities before applying.
Government contracts — Social welfare departments, DV units, and child protection agencies often contract NGOs to deliver services. These are typically multi-year and more stable than foundations but require significant reporting capacity.
Corporate CSR — Target corporates with a stated focus on women, community, or safety. Banks, telecoms, and retail are often active CSR funders. A personal relationship is often more important than a formal proposal.

2.2 The Grant Proposal Structure

Most grant applications ask for variants of the following. Prepare these blocks once and adapt for each funder:

SectionWhat Funders Want to KnowYour Answer (draft here)
Executive Summary Who you are, what you do, how much you need, what it will achieve. (Max 300 words)
Problem Statement What problem does this grant address? Use local data. How many people are affected?
Proposed Program What exactly will you do with the money? Activities, timeline, who delivers them?
Expected Outcomes What will change as a result? Specific, measurable. Use your M&E metrics.
Budget Line-item budget showing how grant funds will be spent. Clear and reasonable.
Organisational Capacity Why should they trust YOU to deliver this? Track record, team, governance.
Sustainability What happens after this grant ends? How do you avoid dependency?

2.3 Grant Writing Tips

Answer the question asked — Don't write what you want to say; write what they asked for. Re-read every question three times before answering.
Use their language — Read the funder's website, annual report, and strategy. Mirror their vocabulary and priorities.
Survivor stories sell — A single anonymised survivor story (with consent) is worth pages of statistics. Lead with humanity, support with data.
Be realistic — Do not promise more than you can deliver. Funders prefer organisations that are honest about constraints over those who over-promise.
Submit early, follow up once — Submit at least 48 hours before the deadline. Follow up once, politely, two weeks after submission if you have not heard.
Part 3 — Individual Donor Cultivation
The 4-touch rule — Donors give when they have been warmed up. Before you ask, touch them at least 4 times: share an impact story, invite to an event, send an update, follow up personally. Then ask.
Thank-you within 48 hours — Every gift, of any size, gets a personal thank-you within 48 hours. For gifts over $500, this is a personal call from the Executive Director. Track in BNB-08b.
Impact reporting to donors — Twice a year (mid-year and year-end), send donors a brief impact update. Not a financial report — a story. "Your donation helped Maria find safe housing and a job." (With consent. Use initials only.)
Recurring giving — A monthly donor giving $50/month is worth $600/year and far more predictable than a one-time $500 gift. Prioritize converting one-time donors to recurring. Make it easy to set up online.
Lapsed donors — Anyone who hasn't given in 12+ months is "lapsed." Re-engage with a personal note acknowledging the gap and sharing an update. Do not just add them to a mass email.

Part 5 — Long-Term Financial Sustainability
Build a 3-month operating reserve — The goal is to have 3 months of operating expenses in a reserve fund. This protects against grant delays, unexpected expenses, and funder changes. Build this over 3–5 years; don't try to do it in year one.
Pursue multi-year grants — Single-year grants create instability. When applying, always ask whether multi-year funding is available. Funders who believe in you will often say yes if you ask early.
Earned income opportunities — Consider whether your organisation can generate income to supplement grants: training other NGOs using the Beacon toolkit, consulting for government, renting space for community events. Earned income should not exceed 20–30% of budget or you risk mission drift.
Board fundraising responsibility — Each board member should either give personally or open doors to donors. A board that doesn't fundraise is a liability. Set a clear board giving expectation at recruitment.
Funder stewardship — Renewing an existing funder costs 10x less effort than winning a new one. Prioritise maintaining current relationships. Submit reports on time, invite funders to your site, send them good news.
