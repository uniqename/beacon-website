1. Founding Vision

The Future We Are Building Toward

The future I am building toward is one where no survivor, regardless of language, location, or financial means, has to navigate trauma alone or in silence. The infrastructure I am building is designed to outlast any single founder — a networked system of digital tools, trained local responders, cultural humility, and survivor-centered care that can operate at scale across Ghana, the United States, and wherever Beacon's reach extends.

Mission

To provide holistic, technology-enabled, culturally grounded support to survivors of domestic violence and exploitation — restoring safety, dignity, and independence for women and children who have experienced trauma.

Dual-Country Model

Beacon operates simultaneously in Ghana (as BeaconPath Ghana, a registered NGO) and the United States (as Beacon of New Beginnings, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit). Both entities share technology infrastructure, brand identity, and strategic leadership while serving distinct communities with contextualised programmes.

3. Five-Year Growth Plan — 2026 to 2031

The five-year plan is built in phases: stabilise, grow, scale, expand, and establish. Each phase unlocks the next only when operational and financial benchmarks are met.

Year 1 — 2026/27

Stabilise

$12,000 – $20,000 operating budget
  • Convert intern to employee; recruit first paid case manager in Ghana
  • Complete case management pilot with 10–15 clients
  • App live on App Store and Google Play; 100+ registered users
  • Establish board (legal, finance, community representation)
  • First formal financial audit / bookkeeping system
  • File first 990 (US) and annual report (Ghana DSW)
Year 2 — 2027/28

Grow

$35,000 – $50,000 operating budget
  • Hire: Programme Coordinator (US), Finance/Admin Officer (Ghana)
  • Expand case management to 30–50 active clients
  • First institutional grant secured ($10,000+)
  • Launch survivor peer-support group programme (in-person, Ghana)
  • US community engagement events; diaspora fundraising campaign
  • Founder begins drawing consistent stipend / salary
Year 3 — 2028/29

Scale

$75,000 – $120,000 operating budget
  • Pilot physical safe space / drop-in centre in Accra (not full shelter — case management model fully validated first)
  • Hire: Trauma-informed counsellor (contractor), Communications manager
  • App: 500+ active users; introduce multilingual support (Twi, Ewe)
  • Formal partnerships with 3+ hospitals / police units (MOUs in place)
  • Second institutional grant; begin applying to USAID / Ford Foundation / UN Women small grants
Year 4 — 2029/30

Expand

$150,000 – $200,000 operating budget
  • Evaluate shelter infrastructure readiness — only proceed if case management model is proven, staffed, and funded
  • Expand to second Ghanaian city (Kumasi or Takoradi)
  • US programme formalised: virtual survivor services, referral network with US shelters
  • Team of 6–8 paid staff across both countries
  • Endowment or operational reserve fund initiated ($25,000 target)
Year 5 — 2030/31

Establish

$250,000 – $350,000 operating budget
  • Full dual-country operations: Ghana (in-person + digital) and US (digital + diaspora programme)
  • 1,000+ survivors served cumulatively; impact data published annually
  • Beacon model documented and replicable — open-source technology, training curriculum
  • Strategic leadership transition planning: infrastructure designed to outlast any single founder
  • Multi-year institutional funding secured; operational reserve ≥ 6 months

6. Anticipated Risks & Mitigation — Founder's Honest Assessment

Honest Mistakes I Anticipate

  • Moving too fast into physical shelter infrastructure before the case management model is fully tested and proven. Physical infrastructure is expensive, hard to reverse, and requires operational capacity that does not yet exist.
  • Underestimating the cultural nuance required to serve Ghanaian and American communities simultaneously without flattening either. Each community has different trauma pathways, help-seeking norms, language needs, and expectations of an organisation like Beacon.
  • Assuming technology adoption will be seamless for users in crisis who may have limited digital literacy or access. The app is powerful but can become a barrier if not introduced with appropriate support, training, and offline fallbacks.
  • Underestimating the personal cost of building two organisations — Beacon and Doxa — in parallel without burning out. Founder sustainability is not a personal indulgence; it is a programme requirement.

What I Am Doing to Get Ahead of These

  • Shelter gate: Physical infrastructure will not be pursued until (a) the case management model has served at least 30 clients successfully, (b) a qualified facility manager is hired, and (c) capital funding is secured without depleting operational reserves.
  • Cultural humility by design: Building a board with Ghana-based and US-based community representatives. Survivor feedback is embedded in every design decision — not collected after launch. Each country has its own configured experience in the app and website.
  • Digital literacy support: Piloting digitally before expanding physically. Case managers will onboard clients onto the app in person. Offline content and emergency numbers are embedded for users without reliable data access.
  • Founder sustainability as policy: Committing to drawing a stipend by Year 2. Board oversight of founder workload. Doxa and Beacon are treated as complementary, not competing — shared administrative infrastructure where possible.

7. Building Infrastructure That Outlasts the Founder

The goal is not to build a founder-dependent organisation. Every system, process, and relationship is designed to be transferable:

  • Technology: Open-architecture app and website hosted on public cloud infrastructure. Code maintained in version control. Documentation written for handover.
  • Governance: Board with clear bylaws, authority, and succession provisions. No single person holds all passwords, accounts, or relationships.
  • Programmes: Case management model documented, piloted, and evaluated before scaling. Training materials written so any qualified hire can deliver the programme.
  • Partnerships: MOUs formalise relationships with hospitals, police, and churches — not dependent on the founder's personal relationships.
  • Financial systems: Accounting, grant reporting, and payroll managed through documented processes, not tribal knowledge.

The infrastructure being built is designed to outlast any single founder — a network of people, tools, and trust strong enough to carry this work forward long after its origin story has been told.
